Creature Behavior, Hunger & Interaction

OTAB expands creature behavior with additional rules for feeding, hunger, hostility, interaction and social behavior.

The goal is not to replace Valheim's creature AI, but to make creatures feel more reactive, more believable and less predictable in situations where vanilla behavior would otherwise feel too simple.

Behavior That Feels Intentional

Vanilla creatures often behave in ways that are technically correct, but also very deterministic and easy to predict.

OTAB builds on that foundation by adding small behavior rules that can change how creatures react depending on hunger, taming state, nearby food, interaction and environmental conditions.

The result is not meant to be realistic for its own sake, but to create behavior that players can observe, understand and plan around.

Feeding Is Separate from Taming

One important OTAB design rule is that eating, being fed, taming and reproduction are not all treated as the same thing.

Creatures may still eat food they find nearby, but only player-dropped food can count for systems such as:

  • taming progress
  • fed timers
  • reproduction eligibility

This prevents creatures from taming or breeding accidentally just because food happened to be nearby.

In OTAB, taming and breeding are meant to remain intentional player actions.

Food Search Feels Less Robotic

In vanilla behavior, creatures usually move to the nearest food in a very predictable way.

OTAB can replace this with a weighted food search system where nearby food is more likely to be chosen, but not always guaranteed.

This makes feeding look less synchronized and helps groups of creatures spread out more naturally.

Hungry and Starving Are Different

OTAB distinguishes between creatures that are simply hungry and creatures that are truly starving.

  • Fed means the fed timer is still active
  • Hungry means the fed timer has expired
  • Starving means the creature has remained hungry for much longer

This allows hunger to become a gradual behavioral change instead of an immediate punishment.

A creature may remain manageable while hungry, but begin to change its behavior once starvation starts.

Starving Creatures Prioritize Food

When a creature becomes starving, OTAB makes food a higher priority than combat.

This means a starving creature may stop attacking and instead rush to consume food on the ground.

This behavior is important because it allows starvation-based hostility to naturally calm down once food becomes available.

Food consumed from the ground can end starvation even if that food does not count as valid feeding for taming or reproduction.

Hungry-driven Hostility Is More Dangerous

A creature that is hungry but not yet starving may still have enough energy to continue fighting instead of looking for food.

Because of this, hunger-based hostility is usually harder to stop than starvation-based hostility.

In practice, this means some creatures may calm down when they are starving and find food, while others may continue fighting as long as they are only hungry.

Situational Hostility

OTAB allows creatures to behave differently depending on their condition.

A creature might:

  • remain peaceful while fed
  • become dangerous when starving
  • tolerate others only under certain conditions
  • behave differently toward players, tamed creatures or wild creatures

This is why some tameable creatures in OTAB are still not fully safe. They may be companions most of the time, but become unstable under stress.

Interaction Can Be Lost When Hunger Starts

Hunger does not only affect hostility. It can also affect how much control the player has over a creature.

Depending on the creature, becoming hungry may disable interactions such as:

  • petting
  • commanding
  • renaming

This gives hunger visible gameplay consequences and prevents feeding from becoming a purely decorative mechanic.

Hungry Creatures May Stop Following Orders

If a creature is currently following a player or staying at a patrol point, OTAB may clear those command states once hunger begins.

In other words, a hungry creature may stop following orders and behave more independently again.

Return to Spawn or Wander Off

After losing commands, creatures do not all behave the same way.

If a creature is still near its original home area, it may return there.

If it is too far away, or if that behavior is disabled for the creature, it may simply begin to wander around on its own.

This reflects the idea that a stressed, hungry or disobedient creature may no longer reliably stay where the player wants it to.

Behavior Comes from Small Combined Rules

OTAB does not rely on one large scripted AI overhaul.

Instead, behavior emerges from several smaller layers such as:

  • feeding state
  • hostility rules
  • interaction permissions
  • reproduction restrictions
  • environmental checks

This allows creatures to feel more varied and more situational without becoming random or impossible to understand.

Why This Matters

OTAB's creature behavior systems are meant to make creatures feel more alive without turning them into chaos machines.

The goal is to create creatures that players can learn, manage and respect — not creatures that can simply be exploited through a few predictable vanilla patterns.
